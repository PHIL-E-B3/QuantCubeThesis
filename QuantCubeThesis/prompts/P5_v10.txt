"sentiment" (string): Hawkish/dovish policy incentive. Values: "strongly_hawkish", "hawkish", "neutral", "dovish", "strongly_dovish".

Polarity by topic:
- inflation: rising/high = hawkish, falling/low = dovish
- labor_market: tight/low unemployment = hawkish, slack/high unemployment = dovish
- economic_activity: stronger growth = hawkish, weaker growth = dovish
- financial_conditions: COUNTERINTUITIVE — easier/accommodative = hawkish (Fed must do more), tighter/stressed = dovish (markets tightened for the Fed)
- monetary_policy: tightening action or signal = hawkish, easing action or signal = dovish

Intensity rules:
- "strongly_hawkish"/"strongly_dovish": requires ONE of the following:
  (a) An explicit intensity marker: surged, soared, plunged, collapsed, sharply, markedly, significantly, substantially, well above/below, far above/below, record high/low, "very strong", "very low", "very weak", "highest/lowest in N years/decades", "tremendous". Intensity markers in comparison phrases also qualify: "substantially stronger than expected", "significantly below forecasts" → strongly_hawkish/dovish.
  (b) A rate move of 50+ bps.
  (c) 3 or more distinct same-direction signals enumerated in a single sentence — count them explicitly. Example: "Economy growing strongly, inflation elevated, and labor market tight" = 3 hawkish signals → strongly_hawkish.
- "hawkish"/"dovish": clear directional signal without the above. "solid" and "robust" stay at hawkish/dovish — too common in Fed prose to signal intensification.
- "neutral": no net directional signal, or opposing signals that cancel.
- Minority views ("one member", "a few participants"): cap at "hawkish"/"dovish", never "strongly_hawkish"/"strongly_dovish".

DEFAULT-TO-NEUTRAL:
- Pure data report with no comparative/directional reference → neutral. "PCE was 2.6% in March" → neutral. Need "above/below target", "stronger/weaker than expected", or an intensity marker to go directional.
- Mere mention of FOMC, policy, rates, balance sheet → neutral unless an explicit action signal is present (raise, cut, tighten, ease, taper, hold announced).
- Deliberation or discussion without action → neutral.
- Tie-break: when uncertain between hawkish/neutral or dovish/neutral, choose NEUTRAL. The signal must be IN the sentence, not inferred from context.

Key rules:
- LEVEL OVERRIDES DIRECTION: "fell but remained high" → hawkish (level governs). "Fell but remained well above target" → strongly_hawkish.
- Negations: label the STATE described. "Not seeing deanchoring" = dovish. "Has not returned to target" = hawkish.
- Holds: "neutral" unless explicitly framed as restrictive (hawkish) or accommodative (dovish).

"horizon" (boolean): true ONLY IF tense = "interpretive" AND explicit long-term language is present: "for some time", "considerable time", "longer run", "over the medium term", "over time", multi-year projections. Otherwise false.

"commitment" (string): If topic excludes "monetary_policy" → always "none". If topic includes "monetary_policy":
- "unconditional" = explicit commitment verb (will/intends to/is prepared to) + specific action, no stated condition.
- "conditional" = BOTH (a) commitment verb + specific action AND (b) explicit condition phrase ("if", "should", "as long as", "until", "depending on"). If either is missing → "none".
- "none" = deliberation, modal verbs alone ("may", "might"), generic data-dependence ("policy will depend on the data"), generic forward guidance without a specific action+condition pair.
TEST: Can you point to BOTH (a) a specific action AND (b) an explicit condition phrase? If either is missing → "none".

"risk" (string): Directional tail asymmetry.
- "skewed_downside": risks toward worse outcomes (growth weakening, labor loosening, financial stress, recession)
- "skewed_upside": risks toward overheating or inflation overshoot. For inflation sentences: skewed_upside means MORE inflation is the tail risk being flagged (e.g. "upside risks to inflation remain", "risk that inflation could re-accelerate"). This label captures the direction of the risk, not whether the outcome is good or bad.
- "symmetric": explicitly balanced ("roughly balanced", "risks on both sides", "risks to both sides of the dual mandate") — do NOT resolve directionally when balanced language is used.
- "na": no explicit risk framing. For conditional/hypothetical structures, populate with the direction of the conditional scenario being warned about.

"width" (string): Epistemic uncertainty or genuine opposing forces.
- "elevated": ANY explicit uncertainty acknowledgement — formal or casual. Triggers include: "uncertain", "uncertainty", "unclear", "hard to gauge", "hard to say", "hard to know", "difficult to assess", "difficult to determine", "we don't know", "cannot know", "imperfectly understood", "unprecedented", "remains to be seen", "it is unclear", "hard to predict", "wide range of outcomes", "difficult to quantify", "we can't be sure", "impossible to know". Bare "uncertain" or "uncertainty" without any modifier qualifies — no intensifier required. ELEVATED FIRES INDEPENDENTLY OF SENTIMENT — do not weaken sentiment because uncertainty is present.
- "contested": two economic forces IN THE SAME SENTENCE, SAME TENSE, each carrying an independent policy signal that pushes in OPPOSITE directions. One force is hawkish in isolation, the other is dovish in isolation. Signals cancel → sentiment = neutral. Connectives: "but", "however", "while", "although", "even as", "despite", "yet".
  ✓ IS contested: "Although household spending rose at a moderate pace, business investment and exports remained soft." (hawkish spending + dovish investment/exports — same topic, opposite forces)
  ✓ IS contested: "Inflation remained above target while labor-market conditions softened." (hawkish inflation + dovish labor — dual-mandate collision)
  ✗ NOT contested: "Fell but remained high" — direction + level on same variable, use level rule.
  ✗ NOT contested: "Growth was solid but uncertainty remains" — a hedge is not an opposing economic force.
- "none": baseline. Modal verbs alone, standard hedges, single-direction signals.
- risk and width can co-exist.
